TITLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
U.S. restrictions on Chinese-made drones (DJI, Autel) and FCC “Covered List” dynamics (2024–2026)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STORY OPENING (narrative hook)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Your drone program used to start with a budget spreadsheet and a Part 107 study guide. In 2025–2026 it also starts with a **compliance spreadsheet**: Who made the radio? Where does telemetry go? Will this model still be importable next year? National-security policy met the farm field and the film set—and fleet owners discovered that “approved to fly today” is not the same as “approved to buy tomorrow.”

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SUMMARY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
U.S. policy moved from agency-level cautions toward harder market restrictions tied to national security, supply-chain integrity, and fears of data exfiltration. Legislative pressure (including NDAA-related mechanisms) and FCC actions increased friction for **new authorizations** for certain foreign manufacturers, while existing fleets in agriculture, filmmaking, and public safety faced uncertain long-term upgrade paths. A pivotal 2025–2026 chapter: broad FCC Covered List placement for foreign UAS (Dec 2025), followed by **narrow exemptions** for Blue UAS–cleared and certain domestic-end-product pathways (Jan 2026)—while DJI remained restricted for new FCC approvals.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
QUESTIONS & ANSWERS (story-style)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: Is every Chinese drone “banned” in the U.S. now?
A: Policy targets **new market access** (FCC authorization, imports, sales of non-exempt models)—not necessarily bricking every aircraft already in hangars. Always verify **current** FCC, NDAA, and state rules; grandfathering and exemptions shifted in early 2026.

Q: Why does the FCC care about drones?
A: Radios and video links need FCC equipment authorization. Putting manufacturers or “foreign-produced UAS” on the **Covered List** blocks new authorizations—functionally a market gate, not just a military procurement rule.

Q: What changed in late 2025 and early 2026?
A: (Verify primary sources before publish.) Press and legal summaries describe: **Dec 22, 2025** — broad addition of foreign-produced UAS and critical components to the Covered List under FY25 NDAA national-security determinations. **Jan 7, 2026** — FCC announced exclusions through Jan 1, 2027 for items on the **Blue UAS Cleared List** and some “domestic end product” categories—benefiting some non-Chinese vendors and component suppliers, not DJI for new approvals.

Q: I run a small ag or survey business—what should I plan for?
A: Document fleet models and firmware policies; budget **refresh cycles** and spare parts; check municipal/insurance vendor requirements; evaluate Blue UAS or U.S./allied alternatives where contracts require it. Treat compliance as a 3–5 year capital plan, not a one-time purchase.

Q: Is this only about China?
A: Framing is geopolitical (PRC-linked supply chains), but implementation is **procedure-based**: covered lists, NDAA sections, federal procurement, state laws, and enterprise security questionnaires—overlapping layers.

Q: How does this connect to the Ukraine/China “dual-use” story?
A: Same global supply chains feed civil and military small UAS. Export controls (story-04), import restrictions (this story), and battlefield demand (story-01) squeeze **components**—ESCs, radios, cameras—not only finished drones.

KEY INSIGHTS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
- Security debate centers on firmware, telemetry, cloud sync, and update channels—not only hardware origin.
- Economic tension: U.S. users often preferred Chinese platforms on price/performance.
- Domestic industrial policy push: incentives for non-Chinese alternatives and U.S. assembly.
- Compliance is layered: federal authorization, federal procurement, state law, insurer/vendor audits.
